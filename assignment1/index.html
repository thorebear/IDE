Four awesome links to visualizations and guides on the web

  1. How Do Major and Degree Affect Lifetime Earnings?
    • Possible to select exactly which (or how much) data to show and compare (out of a huge dataset)
    • Different degrees (bachelor, master, pdf etc.) are easy to separate by they colors.
    • Hovering a line with the mouse gives precise information about what (degree, major, experience level) the line is representing.
    • "Undynamic" colors: E.g. if you compare bachelor degrees for different majors all lines will have the same color.
    • No instructions on how to select multiple majors and/or degrees, without deselecting the rest. It is possible by holding down CTRL, but when no instructions are given it is hard to know.
  2. Google Trends
    • Simple interface, which is easy to understand
    • Highly customizable (select any terms and any number of terms)
    • Good overview to compare search interests on a specific term in different continents and/or countries in the section "Regional interest"
    • No text on how to understand the numbers. E.g. was does the number 100 mean on a country in "Regional interest"?
  3. The PRISM scandal (Snowden menagerie)
    • Interesting subject
    • Interactivity makes you want to 'click' everything
    • Attempts to visualize a very complex topic
    • Densely packed with information including links to articles
    • Sadly fails to provide an overview
    • Visualization is too complex
  4. UniverScale
    • Simple but effective interactivity, zoom away!
    • Compares different scales
    • Some functions does not work as intended
    • Lacks color (or visualization not fully supported by my browser?)

Thorbjørn Christensen

The best Danish football league 'The Danish Superliga' was established in 1991, and currently the best 12 teams in Denmark are participating in the 26th season of the league.

Each season of the league is easily summed up in a table showing the final standing, even the all-time standing (how the teams has performed when combining all 26 seasons) can (and is) summed up in a table. These standings are fine when we are interested in the outcome of a season (who won the title? who was relegated? etc.), but in my opinion they lack an interesting piece of information: How was the route of a team to its final position? Was the bronze winner placed in the lower part of league in first half of the season, and did a magnificent comeback to achieve the bronze medals, or was they placed in the first place in most of the season, just to see their championship dreams falling a part in the last matches?

My idea is the make an interactive view, which show every teams position after each round of any season. It should be possible to select which season to view, and which teams to explorer. Here is a mockup* showing the idea, where a single season and 3 teams selected.

An addition feature could be to combine seasons, and e.g. show the view from round 1 in season 1992/1993 to round 33 in season 1999/2000. If this feature exists, it is important to state how we convert results between seasons, since a victory in some seasons give 2 points and 3 points in other.

All the data exists on SuperStats.dk.

*Only mockups allow two teams to be in the same position after the same number of rounds (Esbjerg and Odense in round 3), the Danish Football Association does not.

Mads Thoudahl

During the danish elections in the early summer 2015, I noted a for me, new tree-ish like visualization communicating where the voters are voting and relations with their voting history. I have added a link to the visualization, here , otherwise click the image below.

Interactivity could be introduced by fine-graining the view to zoom in at geographic areas, educations, institutions, etc.

I imagine the same kind of visualization could produce some very convincing, storytelling of social inheritance, educational level of young citizens, pace of education, stress disorders and other interesting markers, this should be visualized over several years, identifying whether the implemented policies work as intended.

The intended audience should be the politicians and voters, documenting the consequences of those decisions made.

This dataset, is probably collected by 'danmarks statistik' for national data, but local educational institutions, would have to collect and report the data.

Accessing the server

The server used to host the website is a private server located at Mads' home in Helsingør. The server has lots of diskspace available, and enough RAM to launch hundreds of threads simultaneously (4GB), and as it is private, the entire system is at our hands, no quotas. The CPU is not very fast though (Intel Celeron 2 cores, 2.1GHz, 64bit), and no serverfarm is backing up the site on the computational side. The ISP connection could also be a potential bottleneck, if the local network is busy doing other private transactions over the internet. The connection is something like 15/2 Mbps, so just 2 Mbit per second upload. If we refrain from serving videos and the like, the data transactions from this machine should be limited amounts of raw data, the purpose of using javascript is to make the visualizations happen locally on the clients, not the server.

The server is accessed via ssh, and the files are moved to the server by 'pulling' a git repository when logged on to the server. The git repo is hosted on github.
